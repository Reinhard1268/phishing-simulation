# Phishing Awareness Guide
**Company Security Awareness Program | Human Resources & IT Security**

---

## What Is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyberattack where criminals send fraudulent messages — usually emails — that appear to come from trusted sources like your bank, Microsoft, your employer, or a package delivery company. The goal is to trick you into clicking a malicious link, downloading a file, or giving up sensitive information like your password, credit card number, or personal details.

Phishing is the #1 cause of data breaches worldwide. In 2023 alone, phishing attacks accounted for over 36% of all security incidents. The good news is that a few seconds of caution before clicking can stop nearly every phishing attack cold.

---

## The 10 Warning Signs of a Phishing Email

### 1. Urgency or Threats
Phishing emails create panic. Phrases like "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours," "Immediate action required," or "You will lose access" are designed to make you act before you think. Legitimate companies rarely threaten immediate account suspension without prior notice.

**What to do:** Slow down. If it's truly urgent, you can verify by going directly to the company's website — not through the email link.

---

### 2. Sender Address Doesn't Match the Display Name
The "From" name might say "Microsoft Support" but the actual email address is `noreply@microsoft-help-desk-2024.com`. Always click or hover on the sender name to reveal the real email address.

**Red flag example:**
- Display name: `IT Department`
- Actual address: `it-support@company-helpdesk.net` ← not your company's domain

---

### 3. Generic Greetings
Real organizations that have your account information will address you by your name. "Dear Customer," "Dear User," or "Dear Account Holder" are red flags.

---

### 4. Suspicious Links That Don't Match What They Claim
Before clicking any link, hover your mouse over it (don't click!) and look at the URL preview in the bottom of your browser. Ask yourself:
- Does the domain match the company it claims to be from?
- Is it a strange domain like `microsoft.account-verify.xyz` instead of `microsoft.com`?
- Is it a URL shortener like `bit.ly` hiding the real destination?

---

### 5. Spelling and Grammar Errors
Professional organizations proofread their communications. Phishing emails often contain unusual phrasing, awkward grammar, or spelling mistakes — especially for attacks originating outside your country.

---

### 6. Unexpected Attachments
Were you expecting a document from this sender? Attachments with extensions like `.exe`, `.zip`, `.doc` (with macros), `.js`, or `.lnk` are frequently used to deliver malware. Even PDFs can be weaponized.

**Rule:** If you weren't expecting it, don't open it.

---

### 7. Requests for Sensitive Information
No legitimate company will ask you to send your password, Social Insurance Number, banking credentials, or MFA codes by email. If an email asks for this, it is always a scam.

---

### 8. Mismatched Branding
Look carefully at logos, colours, and formatting. Phishers often use slightly wrong logos, different fonts, or off-brand colours. Compare against the real company's official website.

---

### 9. Unusual or Unexpected Requests
CEO fraud ("I need you to buy gift cards urgently"), HR fraud ("Benefits enrollment closing today"), and IT fraud ("Your password expires NOW") are all classic phishing scenarios. If a request is unusual — even if it appears to come from someone you trust — verify through a separate channel before acting.

---

### 10. "Too Good to Be True" Offers
Prize notifications, unclaimed refunds, and job offers with unusually high salaries are bait. If it sounds too good, it almost certainly is.

---

## How to Check If an Email Is Legitimate

Use this 5-step checklist before clicking anything:

**Step 1:** Check the sender's full email address (not just the display name).

**Step 2:** Hover over all links — do the URLs match the expected domain?

**Step 3:** Ask yourself: "Was I expecting this email?" If not, be extra cautious.

**Step 4:** Look for urgency or pressure tactics — legitimate senders don't usually demand instant action.

**Step 5:** If still unsure, go directly to the official website by typing the URL manually, or call the sender using a phone number from their official website — not one provided in the email.

---

## What To Do If You Receive a Phishing Email

1. **Do not click any links** or open any attachments.
2. **Do not reply** to the email.
3. **Report it** by forwarding it to `soc@company.local` or using the "Report Phishing" button in your email client.
4. **Delete the email** from your inbox and trash folder.
5. If the email came from someone you know, contact them through a different channel (phone, Teams) to warn them their account may be compromised.

---

## What To Do If You Accidentally Clicked

**Don't panic — your quick action matters.**

1. **Disconnect your device from the network immediately** (unplug ethernet or turn off Wi-Fi).
2. **Do not enter any credentials** on the page you landed on.
3. **Call IT Security immediately**: ext. 4357 or `soc@company.local`.
4. **Do not reboot your computer** — this can destroy forensic evidence.
5. IT will tell you what to do next. You will not be penalized for reporting promptly.

## Real vs. Fake: Side-by-Side Examples

| Real Microsoft Email | Phishing Email |
|---------------------|----------------|
| From: `account@microsoft.com` | From: `security@microsoft-account-alerts.com` |
| Addressed to your name | "Dear User" |
| Link goes to `login.microsoftonline.com` | Link goes to `microsoft.login-secure-verify.xyz` |
| No urgency pressure | "Act within 24 hours or lose access" |
| No request for password | "Verify your password to continue" |

## Reporting Procedure

| How to Report | Where |
|---------------|-------|
| Email (forward suspected phish) | `soc@company.local` |
| Internal ticketing | IT Portal > Security > Report Phishing |
| Phone (urgent/if you clicked) | ext. 4357 |
| Slack | #report-phishing channel |

**There is no penalty for reporting** — even if it turns out to be a false alarm. Reporting saves the organization and protects your colleagues.
